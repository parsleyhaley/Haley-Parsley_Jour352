A Train Derailment Caused Flames "Three Stories Tall." It Could Have Been Worse.

by Haley Parsley

May 19, 2025

Image by Doug Zink

The derailment illustrates the dangers posed by the rail industry's continued use of dangerous tank cars.

On July 5, 2024, Kevin Wolsky’s day began with a call from his local sheriff.

It was around 5:30 a.m. when the North Dakota farmer picked up the phone. “We had a derailment,” he remembers the sheriff saying. “I need to know if your mother or anyone is home.”

Wolsky is the third generation to run his family’s farm, which sits about 300 yards from a railroad line that cuts across southern North Dakota. He no longer lives on the farm, but his mother does.

Concerned for her safety, Wolsky jumped in his truck and raced over to the house. Within half an hour, he stood before the first train derailment Bordulac had seen since 1989. “It had flames three stories tall,” he said, during a phone interview.

First responders at the scene of the derailment around 9 a.m., July 5, 2024. Credit: Kevin Wolsky

Wolsky would later learn that at around 3:30 that morning, a freight train run by the Canadian Pacific Kansas City Railroad had derailed while passing over a creek near Wolsky’s farm, in the small town of Bordulac.

The force of impact caused the cars to slam into each other and yank and twist against their couplers. Twenty-nine cars from the train jumped the track, some toppling over on their sides, according to the National Transportation Safety Board, a federal agency that investigates commercial transportation accidents. At least nine tank cars carrying hazardous materials were torn apart or punctured, and various pieces of equipment were shorn off their sides. The contents of the cars, methanol and anhydrous ammonia, almost immediately began seeping into the surrounding land.

Destroyed tank cars recovered from the scene of the accident. The car in the forfront of the image contained methanol, a highly flammable chemical. Credit: Kevin Wolsky

Methanol and anhydrous ammonia are considered highly flammable chemicals; methanol is a liquid and anhydrous ammonia is a gas. As the methanol pooled beneath the derailment, it was heated by the train and quickly ignited. As the flames grew they heated the anhydrous ammonia, which also began to burn. The fallen tank cars that had not been compromised began to heat, creating internal pressure. Soon, nine other cars had failed, adding their contents to the toxic pools around them and feeding the fire.

Wolsky was able to help his mother to safety, and she remained evacuated from her home for two days before returning, after the fire had subsided. None were injured, and surrounding farms were not damaged, in part, resident Doug Zink said, because the wind blew the smoke and fire South of the derailment.

A new investigation by the Howard Center revealed that the derailment could have been far more devastating, had conditions been different that day.

Tank cars damaged during the derailment. The car furthest to the right contained methanol. The car second from the right contained anhydrous ammonia. Credit: Kevin Wolsky

Railroads are not required to provide the public with information about the hazardous materials they carry, even if those materials travel through regular people’s backyards every day. After a derailment, railroads are only required to provide information about the contents of the train that were released during the accident. The full makeup of the train—called the consist—is rarely made public. Following the derailment in Bordulac, CPKC reported that, the twenty-nine derailed cars, 17 were transporting hazardous materials.

However, data provided to the Howard Center by RailState, a private company that uses sensors to collect detailed information about the movement of freight trains and their cargo, shows that the full consist included at least 51 cars loaded with the most dangerous, and most flammable, hazardous materials, including liquified petroleum gas and ethanol.

At least one of the derailed cars was a model widely considered by safety experts to be unsafe for use transporting flammable hazardous materials like methanol and anhydrous ammonia. In a 2015 report, the NTSB said that the tank cars’ weak design, “create[s] the risk that, in an accident, hazardous materials could be released and, in the case of flammable liquids such as crude oil and ethanol, could ignite and cause catastrophic damage.”

>In 2015, the FAST Act required that DOT-111 tank cars be phased-out for use carrying certain types of hazardous materials, including crude oil and ethanol, dangerous gasses, and flammable liquids. DOT-111 cars must be replaced, the law states, by a newer, reinforced model, known as the DOT-117. The FAST ACT introduced a phase-out schedule that is staggered by material. Most of the deadlines have passed, but one is remaining: a May 2029 deadline for the phase-out of DOT-111 cars and their replacement with DOT-117 cars.

Safety officials, including the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration, believe that this deadline is too late, and that it would be feasible for the tank car industry to complete the phase-out more quickly.

Tank car owners and shippers should carefully and closely consider whether their decisions place financial considerations ahead of safety and protection of human lives and the environment.

In 2023, PHMSA put out an advisory urging tank car owners and chemical shippers to stop using DOT-111 cars. “Tank car owners and shippers should carefully and closely consider whether their decisions place financial considerations ahead of safety and protection of human lives and the environment.”

But those financial considerations are often more compelling to shippers that PHMSA’s pleas, according to Paul Stancil, an NTSB investigator who helped develop the safer DOT-117 model. “A lot of the pushback that has occurred here comes from shippers or fleet owners who are looking at it from an economic viewpoint,” he said, during an interview in March. “Several have financial interest in large fleets of these, I'll call substandard tank cars, and they're trying to maximize the return on their investments.”
